01The big picture

The whole project is a rehearsal of one real consulting job: move a company's data from an old system to a modern one, and prove it arrived intact.

That's it. Everything else — Postgres, DuckDB, dbt, the harness — is machinery in service of those two verbs: move the data, then prove the move was faithful. Most of the dollar value in migration work is in the second verb. Anyone can copy rows; the client is paying for confidence that nothing was lost, duplicated, or quietly changed along the way.

02Why it has to be a simulation

In a real engagement, the client hands you their legacy system and you migrate off it. You can't practice that at home — you don't have a client's old database sitting around. So the lab fakes both ends of the migration and lets you play every role at once:

In the lab

DuckDB (or a Snowflake trial)

The Olist e-commerce dataset

dbt models

You are simultaneously the company that owns the data and the consultant migrating it. That dual role is why you have to both "break" things and "catch" them later — you're manufacturing the exact situation a real client would bring you.

03The three zones

Data moves through three stages, each a distinct place it lives. This layering is standard in modern warehouses, so getting it in your hands is itself a skill.

Zone 1 — Raw landing (source)

The CSVs loaded into Postgres verbatim, no cleaning. This is the messy reality you're migrating off. Legacy systems are full of inconsistent dates, nullable fields, and free text — and that mess is exactly what you'll later prove you carried across faithfully.

Zone 2 — Migrated landing (target)

An untouched copy of that raw data, now sitting in the new warehouse as raw_* tables. Still no transformation. The only thing that happened is the data crossed systems. Keeping this copy unmodified is deliberate — see the next section.

Zone 3 — Marts (transformed)

The cleaned, reshaped, business-ready tables that dbt builds on top of Zone 2. This is what dashboards and analysts actually read. A fct_orders table with one tidy row per order, enriched with totals and delivery times, lives here.

04Why you load raw first (the "ELT" idea)

You might expect to clean data while moving it — that's the old approach, called ETL (Extract, Transform, Load: transform on the way in). The modern approach flips the last two: ELT (Extract, Load, then Transform after it's landed).

Analogy
Moving houses. ETL is unpacking and arranging every box in the moving truck before carrying anything inside — if you misplace something, it's already lost in transit and you can't tell whether the breakage happened in your old house or the truck. ELT is carrying every box inside untouched first, confirming they all arrived, then unpacking room by room. If a lamp's broken, you still have it, and you know exactly where to look.

The concrete reason: if you clean and reshape data during the move and a bug appears, you've corrupted your only copy and can't tell whether the fault is in the move or the cleanup. Keeping a raw, unmodified copy in Zone 2 means you can always re-derive everything from a known-good starting point, and you can validate the move independently of the transformation. ELT won broadly because cloud-warehouse storage and compute got cheap enough that landing everything first is no longer wasteful.

Why it matters for you
"Load first, transform after, and keep an immutable raw copy" is something you can say in any interview and have it mark you as someone who's done this properly.

05What dbt is actually doing

dbt is the tool that builds Zone 3 from Zone 2. In practice it lets you write transformations as plain SELECT statements in SQL files, and it handles the order they run in, tests them, and draws the dependency graph. Two layers matter:

Staging models

One per source table, kept 1:1 with the raw data. They do only light, safe work: rename cryptic columns to clean names, cast text into proper dates and numbers. No joins, no business logic. Think of staging as a contract — "here is the clean, typed version of this one source table" — that everything downstream can rely on.

Marts (facts and dimensions)

This is where business logic lives. The vocabulary comes from dimensional modeling (Kimball), the dominant way to structure analytics tables:

TermPlain meaningOlist example
Fact tableThe events/measurements — the "verbs." Lots of rows, numbers you sum.fct_orders: one row per order, with totals
DimensionThe descriptive context — the "nouns" you slice by.dim_customers, dim_products
GrainWhat exactly one row represents. The single most important modeling decision."one row per order" vs "one row per order line"
Star schemaFacts in the center, dimensions around them like points of a star.orders fact joined out to customer/product/date dims

The reason "grain" gets hammered: if you join an orders table to its line-items carelessly, one order with three items becomes three rows, and every total you compute is now triple-counted. Defining and protecting the grain is what keeps the numbers honest.

06The problem the harness solves

You just moved 100,000 orders and 112,000 line-items from the old system to the new one. How do you actually know it worked?

This is the entire reason the harness exists. After a migration, "looks fine" is not an answer you can give a client — especially in healthcare or finance, where being wrong has consequences and auditors want evidence. The harness is a tool that compares the source and the target from several independent angles and produces a pass/fail report you can attach to a go-live decision. It turns "trust me" into "here's the proof."

07Why just counting rows isn't enough

The obvious check is "count the rows on both sides — if they match, we're good." That's necessary but dangerously incomplete.

The trap
Suppose the migration dropped 50 rows and accidentally duplicated 50 others. The total count is identical on both sides. The row-count check passes. The data is corrupt. You sign off, the client goes live, and weeks later their finance team finds revenue is wrong — and it traces back to you.

The lesson is that any single check has blind spots. The fix isn't a smarter single check — it's several independent checks, each catching a class of error the others miss. That's why the harness runs seven. If counts, sums, distinct counts, grouped counts, and content hashes all agree, the odds of an undetected corruption are vanishingly small.

08The seven checks, one by one

Each row below is a different lens. The "catches what others miss" column is the point — it's why you run all of them, not just the first.

CheckQuestion it asksCatches what others miss
Row countDid the same number of rows arrive?Gross loss or duplication. (Blind to offsetting errors.)
Aggregate (sum)Does sum(price) match to the penny?The offsetting-error trap above, and silent value drift — e.g. a script that multiplied price by 1.01. Row count wouldn't budge; the sum would. Run at zero tolerance for money.
Distinct countSame number of unique customers?Entities getting merged or split by a bad join in the migration.
Grouped countSame row counts broken down by status?Where the problem is. A plain count says "100 missing"; this says "all 100 were shipped orders" — pointing straight at the broken piece.
Hash totalDoes a checksum of each row's contents match?Field values silently changing (a status flipped, a date shifted) while counts and sums still tie out.
Referential integrityDoes every line-item still point at a real order?Broken parent-child links that surface later as orphaned records.
Null rateDid the share of empty values stay stable?Column-mapping mistakes — the migration grabbed the wrong source field, or dropped one.
The one-sentence version
A migration can pass row counts and still be wrong. The grouped count tells you where it broke; the sum and the hash catch changes that counts can't see. That sentence is your sales pitch.

09Why it's "config-driven" (and why that's the IP)

The checks aren't written as one-off Python for the Olist tables. They're listed in a YAML config file — plain text describing each check. To validate a brand-new table on a different client, you add a few lines of config; you don't write new code.

# adding a check is adding data, not code:
- name: invoices_total
  type: aggregate
  agg: sum
  column: amount
  source_table: invoices
  target_table: raw_invoices
  tolerance: 0.0

This is the difference between a script (works once, for one job) and a harness (a reusable framework you bring to every engagement). It's why this is worth treating as sellable intellectual property rather than throwaway glue: you build the engine once, and every future client is a new config file plus a report with their logo on it.
