01Two axes, not one list

Your materials look like ten separate subjects — modeling, migration, orchestration, governance, BI. They aren't. They're one pipeline read along two axes:

  • The data path (horizontal) — the journey every byte takes from an app to a dashboard. Five stages, in order.
  • The cross-cutting concerns (vertical) — things that apply at every stage at once: orchestration, quality, governance, cost. They don't sit in the flow; they wrap it.

This is the data engineering lifecycle: a five-stage flow plus a set of undercurrents that touch all five. It is the canonical framing in your Architecture reading and the one interviewers score you against. [Concepts · Part 07]

02The five stages — each answers one question

Don't memorize tools. For each stage, hold the one load-bearing question it exists to answer. Everything in the docs is an answer to one of these.

StageThe one question it answersLives in
1 · SourceWhere does data originate, and in what shape? (row-oriented OLTP, built for writes, not analytics)Foundations A
2 · Ingest / MigrateHow do we move it without losing or corrupting it — and prove we didn't? (ELT vs ETL, CDC, the reconciliation ladder)Concepts 02 · your differentiator
3 · StoreWhere does it land so analytics is fast and cheap? (storage ÷ compute, warehouse / lake / lakehouse, table formats, partitioning)Concepts 01
4 · TransformHow do we turn raw rows into trusted, modeled tables? (dbt staging→marts, declare the grain, star schema, SCD2)Concepts 03
5 · ServeHow do consumers get one agreed answer? (semantic/metrics layer, governed dashboards, row-level security)Concepts 03 · 06

Notice the order is also the order of your itinerary: SQL/foundations → modeling → dbt → quality → migration → orchestration → cloud → semantic/BI. You're walking the spine left to right, deepening one stage at a time. [Itinerary · sequencing]

03The undercurrents — why they're drawn across, not in line

These four don't happen at a stage; they happen to all stages. That's the whole reason to draw them as bands underneath rather than boxes in the flow. Putting governance "after BI" or quality "in transform only" is the classic junior mistake — it's a band, not a box.

UndercurrentThe jobConcrete handle from the docs
OrchestrationRun the stages in the right order, on time, recoverablyIdempotency + backfills; asset-centric (Dagster) vs task-centric (Airflow)
Quality & ObservabilityCatch bad data before it reaches a human7 quality dimensions; 5 pillars; dbt tests; a data contract that fails the build
GovernanceMake access and use legal and safeRBAC vs ABAC; column masking; lineage; PII classification; HIPAA/SOX
Cost / FinOpsKeep it affordable as a design choiceBytes scanned — partition + cluster to scan less
The two jobs of migrationWhen you reach Stage 2, hold this: a migration is two jobs, and the second is the hard one — (1) move the data, (2) prove it arrived intact. Job 2 is the reconciliation ladder (count → checksum → row-diff) and it's your stated differentiator. We build it the hard way — rough Python first — in a later lesson. 04Practice — retrieval, not recognition

Reading the diagram feels like learning; it isn't. Retrieval is. Cover the diagram above, then do these from memory. Flip each card only after you've answered out loud.

Name the five stages of the data path, in order.

Source → Ingest/Migrate → Store → Transform → Serve. (Generation → Ingestion → Storage → Transformation → Serving.)

Name the four cross-cutting undercurrents and the one-word job of each.

Orchestration (sequencing), Quality & Observability (trust), Governance (safety/legality), Cost/FinOps (affordability). Drawn as bands because they touch every stage.

A migration is two jobs. What are they, and which is harder?

(1) Move the data; (2) prove it arrived intact via reconciliation. Job 2 — proving correctness — is the hard one and the consulting differentiator.

Every prompt below is one spot on the spine. Pick it.
