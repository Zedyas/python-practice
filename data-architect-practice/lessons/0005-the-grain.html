The grain — what one row means

Before any star schema, any SUM, any dbt model: name what one row represents. Get the grain wrong and every number downstream is wrong. We'll use healthcare claims, because claims data has a built-in multi-level grain that catches everyone.

The win

State the grain of any table as "one row = one ______"; read the claims grain hierarchy and the double-count trap that comes from mixing grains; and connect it to the thing it actually builds — a fact table is just measures recorded at one declared grain, surrounded by the dimensions that describe it.

01What grain is Bedrock → Transform

Definition: the grain of a table is what a single row represents — its level of detail. You declare it by finishing one sentence:

one row = one ______

"Member-level" and "claim-level" are the everyday way of saying it, and they're correct — but always pin them to the "one row per ___" form, because that's what removes ambiguity:

  • Member-level → "one row per member"
  • Claim-level → "one row per claim"
Why insist on the phrasing"Claim-level" is ambiguous in real claims data — a claim is a header with service lines underneath it, so "claim-level" could mean one row per claim or one row per line. That ambiguity is exactly where the bugs live. The "one row per ___" form forces you to say which.

02The claims grain hierarchy Transform

A healthcare claim isn't one thing — it's a header with lines beneath it. So "claims data" actually spans several grains, and each belongs in its own table:

TableGrain — "one row per…"Unique keyWhat lives here
Member / eligibilityone member (per enrollment span)member_id (+ span)name, DOB, plan, PCP — a dimension
Claim headerone claimclaim_idsubmitted/paid date, billing provider, claim_total
Claim lineone service line on a claim(claim_id, line_no)one CPT/procedure, units, line_amount
Member-monthone member per month(member_id, month)a rolled-up summary grain (PMPM metrics)

So "claim-level" splits into claim-header grain (one row per claim) and claim-line grain (one row per procedure on the claim). A claim with an office visit + a lab + an x-ray is 1 header row and 3 line rows.

03The double-count trap Quality · the #1 grain bug

This is the bug that makes grain matter, and it's the thing an interviewer is testing when they ask "what's the grain?". Take claim C-100, member M-1, three lines at $150 each → claim_total $450.

-- claim_header: one row per CLAIM        claim_line: one row per SERVICE LINE
--   C-100  M-1  $450   (1 row)             C-100  line 1  $150
--                                          C-100  line 2  $150
--                                          C-100  line 3  $150

-- CORRECT — sum each measure at its own grain
SELECT sum(line_amount) FROM claim_line   WHERE claim_id='C-100';   -- 450
SELECT sum(claim_total) FROM claim_header WHERE claim_id='C-100';   -- 450

-- BUG — header amount stamped onto every line by the join, then summed
SELECT sum(h.claim_total)
FROM claim_header h JOIN claim_line l USING (claim_id)
WHERE h.claim_id='C-100';                                -- 1350  ← 450 × 3 lines!

Joining a header (1 row) to its lines (N rows) is a fan-out: it multiplies any header-level amount by N. That gives you the rule the whole lesson hangs on:

The ruleA table holds one grain, and every measure in it must be true at that grain. claim_total is true at claim grain, not line grain — so it must not live on the line rows. Mixing grains in one table is the cardinal sin. When you must combine them, aggregate to a common grain before you sum.

04The grain is the primary key Transform

The grain and the unique key are the same statement said two ways. Claim-line grain → the key is (claim_id, line_no). This gives you a sharp diagnostic:

DiagnosticIf you believe a table is claim-grain but two rows share a claim_id, your real grain is finer than you thought. Test it: count(*) vs count(DISTINCT claim_id). If they differ, claim_id is not the grain — and any join or SUM that assumed it was is already wrong.

So "what's the grain?" and "what column(s) make a row unique?" are one question. If you can't answer it, you don't understand the table yet.

05Build at the atomic grain Transform

The atomic grain is the finest level your source supports — here, the claim line. Model your facts there, and aggregate up when you need to (line → claim → member-month).

The reason is one-directional: you can always roll up, never split back down. Store at line grain and you can still answer claim totals, member totals, PMPM, per-procedure spend. Pre-aggregate to member-month and the procedure detail is gone forever — you've thrown away every question you didn't anticipate.

Interview line"I model facts at the atomic grain — the finest the source gives me — because aggregation is one-way: I can always roll detail up, but I can never recover detail I pre-summed away."

06Bringing it back to fact tables Transform · the payoff

Here's where grain stops being trivia and becomes the backbone of dimensional modeling. A fact table is nothing but measures recorded at one declared grain, surrounded by the dimensions that describe that grain. Unpack that sentence:

The fact table = the grain, made concrete

Our claim-line fact, fct_claim_line, is the sentence "one row per service line":

-- fct_claim_line  ·  grain: one row per service line
claim_id, line_no            -- the grain / primary key
member_key, provider_key,    -- foreign keys → dimensions
date_key, plan_key, cpt_key
line_amount, allowed_amt,    -- the measures (facts)
paid_amt, units

Two kinds of columns, and grain decides both: the keys (what identifies and describes a row) and the measures (the numbers you aggregate).

Dimensions describe the grain

Every foreign key points to a dimension — a table at one-row-per-entity grain that gives the row its context: dim_member (one per member), dim_date (one per day), dim_provider, dim_cpt (one per procedure code). A central fact surrounded by these dimension tables is a star schema — and the grain of the fact is the star's defining statement.

Grain decides which dims can attachA dimension can join to a fact only if it's meaningful at or above the fact's grain. dim_cpt attaches to fct_claim_line (each line has exactly one procedure) — but it could not attach to a claim-header fact, because a claim has many procedures and "the CPT of a claim" isn't a single value. Push to a finer fact and more dimensions become attachable. That's another reason to model atomic.

Measures must be additive at the grain

The double-count trap from §03 is really a statement about measures. A measure is additive if summing it across every dimension is meaningful at the fact's grain:

  • Additive — paid_amt, line_amount: sum across member, date, provider, procedure — all valid. The well-behaved case.
  • Semi-additive — a balance or a member_months count: additive across some dimensions but not time (you sum enrollment across members, but summing a month-end balance across months is nonsense — you average or take period-end).
  • Non-additive — a ratio or rate (paid_per_visit): never sum it; recompute it from its additive parts at the grain you want.

And claim_total on a line row? Non-additive at line grain — which is the §03 bug stated in dimensional-modeling language.

The three fact-table shapes — each a grain choice

Kimball's three fact types are really three answers to "what's the grain?":

Fact typeGrainClaims example
Transactionone row per eventfct_claim_line — one row per service line. Finest, most common.
Periodic snapshotone row per entity per periodfct_member_month — one row per member per month (PMPM, enrollment).
Accumulating snapshotone row per process instance, updated over its lifefct_claim_lifecycle — one row per claim, with milestone dates (submitted → adjudicated → paid) filled in as it moves.
The throughlineKimball's 4-step design is: (1) pick the business process, (2) declare the grain, (3) choose the dimensions, (4) choose the facts. Step 2 is load-bearing — steps 3 and 4 are only answerable once the grain is fixed. That's why "what's the grain?" is the first question you ask of any fact table.

07Practice — say it before you flip

Define grain in the one form that removes ambiguity.

"One row = one ______." Finish the sentence and you've declared the grain (e.g. "one row per service line").

A claim has 3 service lines. How many rows at header grain vs line grain?

1 row at claim-header grain; 3 rows at claim-line grain. Header = one per claim, line = one per procedure on the claim.

Why does SUM(claim_total) over a header-joined-to-lines query inflate?

Fan-out: the join repeats the header's $450 on each of the 3 lines, so the sum becomes 450×3 = 1350. claim_total is non-additive at line grain.

Why model at the atomic grain?

Aggregation is one-way — you can roll detail up but never recover detail you pre-summed away. The atomic grain preserves every future question.

In one sentence, what is a fact table?

Measures recorded at one declared grain, surrounded by the dimension tables that describe that grain (a star schema).
